### 1. 529 College Savings Plan - "King" of Education Savings

**Concepts Taught**:

**Tax Benefits:**
- Contributions: After-tax (no federal deduction, many states give state tax deduction)
- Growth: TAX-FREE (no taxes on investment gains)
- Withdrawals: TAX-FREE if used for qualified education expenses
- Penalty: Income tax + 10% penalty on earnings if used for non-education

**Contribution Limits:**
- Very high aggregate limits ($300K-$500K+ per beneficiary)
- No annual contribution limit (but gift tax considerations)
- Anyone can contribute (parents, grandparents, relatives, friends)

**Qualified Expenses:**
- College: tuition, fees, room & board, books, computers
- K-12 private school: up to $10K/year (since 2017)

**Flexibility:**
- Can change beneficiary to another family member (tax-free, penalty-free)
- Can use at any accredited U.S. college or many abroad
- NEW 2024: Can roll up to $35,000 to beneficiary's Roth IRA

**Gift Tax Rules:**
- Annual gift tax exclusion (2024-2025): $18,000 per person
- 5-year superfunding: $90,000 (single) or $180,000 (married couple)
- Must file Form 709, cannot give other gifts to that person for 5 years

**State Tax Benefits:**
- Most states offer state income tax deduction ($2K-$10K/year typically)
- Usually must use own state's plan to get deduction

**Investment Options:**
- Age-based portfolios (automatic rebalancing from stocks ‚Üí bonds)
- Similar to target-date retirement funds

### 3. Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)

**Concepts Taught**:

**Tax Treatment:**
- Same as 529: After-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for education

**Key Differences from 529:**

**Contribution Limits:**
- **ONLY $2,000 per year per beneficiary** (much lower than 529!)
- All contributors combined cannot exceed $2,000
- Contributions end when child turns 18

**Income Limits:**
- Phase-out: $95K-$110K (single), $190K-$220K (married)
- High earners locked out completely

**Qualified Expenses:**
- K-12 expenses (more flexible than 529)
- College expenses (same as 529)

**Age Limit:**
- Must use funds by age 30 or pay penalty (529 has no age limit)

**CFP Perspective:** Less popular due to $2,000/year limit - insufficient for most college savings

### 5. UTMA/UGMA Custodial Accounts

**Concepts Taught**:

**What it is:**
- UTMA = Uniform Transfers to Minors Act
- UGMA = Uniform Gifts to Minors Act
- "Give money directly to child, parent manages until age 18-21"

**Tax Treatment:**
- **NO special tax benefits** (regular taxable account)
- Investment gains taxed annually
- "Kiddie Tax" rules apply

**Ownership:**
- **Money belongs to THE CHILD** (not parents!)
- Irrevocable - can't take it back
- Child gets full control at age of majority (18-21)

**The BIG Risk:**
- Child turns 18, gets $50K, spends on sports car instead of college
- Parents have ZERO control

**Financial Aid Impact:**
- Counts as **student's asset** ‚Üí Reduces aid by up to 20% of value
- 529 counts as parent's asset ‚Üí Only reduces aid by ~5.64%

**CFP Recommendation:** Most planners avoid UTMA/UGMA for large education savings due to:
- No tax benefits
- Loss of control
- Worse financial aid impact

---

### 6. Roth IRA Creative Strategy

**Concepts Taught**:

**How it works:**
- Contribute to parent's own Roth IRA
- **Contributions** can be withdrawn anytime tax-free, penalty-free
- **Earnings** can be withdrawn penalty-free (but taxed) for education

**Example:**
- Contribute $30K over 10 years
- Grows to $50K ($30K + $20K earnings)
- Withdraw $30K contributions: tax-free, penalty-free
- Withdraw $20K earnings: penalty-free, but taxed

**Pros:**
- Dual purpose: college OR retirement
- Financial aid: retirement accounts don't count in FAFSA
- Flexibility: not locked into education

**Cons:**
- Lower limits ($7K/year, or $8K if age 50+)
- Income limits for eligibility
- Takes away from retirement savings

**CFP Perspective:** Backup strategy only - ONLY if already maxing out retirement AND have extra

**Critical Principle:** Retirement comes FIRST! (B.13 principle)
