### Social Security Claiming Strategy - Detailed Rules

#### Full Retirement Age (FRA) by Birth Year
- Born 1943-1954: FRA = 66
- Born 1955-1959: FRA = 66 + 2 months per year (gradual increase from 66 to 67)
- Born 1960 or later: FRA = 67

#### Early Claiming Penalty (Age 62)
- Claim at age 62 with FRA 67 = **30% permanent reduction** (receive 70% of full benefit)
- Each month early = roughly 5/9 of 1% reduction (first 36 months), then 5/12 of 1% for additional months
- **Permanent reduction** - not temporary!

#### Delayed Retirement Credits (Age 70)
- Each year delay past FRA = **8% increase** in benefits
- FRA 67 → Age 70 = 3 years × 8% = **24% increase** (receive 124% of full benefit)
- **No benefit to waiting past age 70!** (credits stop at 70)

#### Breakeven Age
- Typically around **age 78-82** depending on FRA
- Live past breakeven → delaying wins (higher monthly benefit × more months)
- Die before breakeven → claiming early wins (lower monthly benefit but more total months)

### CFP Professional Guidance - When to Claim

#### When to Consider Claiming EARLY (Age 62-63)
1. **Poor health/shorter life expectancy** - Won't reach breakeven age (78-82)
2. **Immediate financial need** - Unemployed, can't work, need income now
3. **Higher-earning spouse delays** - Lower earner claims early, higher earner delays to maximize survivor benefit (spousal strategy)
4. **Investment opportunity** - Can invest early benefits and earn strong returns (though risky strategy, not usually recommended)

#### When to DELAY (to Age 70)
1. **Good health/longevity in family** - Likely to live past breakeven age
2. **Still working** - Earnings test penalty applies before FRA (would lose benefits anyway)
3. **Survivor protection** - Higher earner delays so surviving spouse gets larger benefit (critical for married couples)
4. **Other income sources** - Don't need Social Security yet, let it grow at guaranteed 8%/year
5. **Inflation hedge** - Higher base benefit = higher COLA adjustments each year

### Social Security Earnings Test Penalty

**New Concept Introduced** (student indicated: "不清楚" - not clear)

#### The Rule
If you claim Social Security **before Full Retirement Age (FRA)** AND you're still working, Social Security will **reduce your benefits** if your earnings exceed certain limits.

#### 2025 Earnings Test Limits (exam numbers to memorize)

**Before FRA:**
- Earnings limit: ~$22,320/year ($1,860/month)
- Penalty: Lose **$1 in benefits for every $2 earned** over the limit
- Example: Earn $32,320 → $10,000 over limit → lose $5,000 in benefits

**The year you REACH FRA:**
- Earnings limit: ~$59,520/year (much higher!)
- Penalty: Lose **$1 for every $3 earned** over limit (less harsh, only 1:3 ratio)
- Only applies to months BEFORE your birthday month

**After FRA:**
- **NO earnings limit!** Work and earn unlimited income, keep full Social Security benefits
- This is why many people wait until FRA to claim if still working

#### Important Clarification
- Benefits aren't truly "lost forever" - Social Security recalculates at FRA to give you credit back
- But it creates **cash flow problems** in the short term
- For CFP exam purposes: if working before FRA with high earnings, advise client to delay claiming

### Medicare Coverage - Complete Mastery

#### Medicare Part A - Hospital Insurance
**Covers:** Inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing (short-term), hospice, home health (limited)
**Does NOT cover:** Long-term care, dental, vision, hearing
**Cost:** $0/month (if worked 40+ quarters / 10 years), ~$1,632 deductible per stay

#### Medicare Part B - Medical Insurance
**Covers:** Doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive screenings, medical equipment
**Does NOT cover:** Prescriptions (need Part D), dental, vision, hearing, long-term care
**Cost:** ~$174.70/month standard, higher for high earners (IRMAA)
- Single income > $103,000 → surcharge
- Married income > $206,000 → surcharge

#### Medicare Part C - Medicare Advantage
**What it is:** Alternative to Original Medicare (A+B), offered by private insurers
**Includes:** Parts A + B + usually D, often extras (dental, vision, fitness)
**Trade-off:** Lower costs but restricted provider networks

### Medicare Advantage vs Medigap - Complete Clarification

#### The Two Paths (Must Choose ONE):

**Path 1: Original Medicare + Optional Medigap**
- Enroll in Parts A + B (Original Medicare)
- Add Part D (prescriptions)
- Optionally add Medigap to cover gaps (deductibles, copays)
- **Pros:** See any doctor, no networks, maximum flexibility
- **Cons:** Higher costs, more complex (3+ separate plans)

**Path 2: Medicare Advantage (Part C)**
- Enroll in Part A + B first (required)
- Then switch to Part C (private plan replaces A+B)
- Part C usually includes Part D (prescriptions)
- **CANNOT add Medigap** (illegal to sell Medigap to Part C enrollees)
- **Pros:** Lower premiums, extra benefits (dental/vision), simpler (one plan)
- **Cons:** Must use network doctors, need referrals, less flexibility

**CRITICAL CFP RULE:** Original Medicare + Medigap OR Medicare Advantage (Part C) - NEVER BOTH!
