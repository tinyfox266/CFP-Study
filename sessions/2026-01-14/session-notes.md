### 1. Long-Term Care Insurance - Detailed Features

#### Four Key Policy Features:

**1.1 Daily Benefit Amount**
- How much insurance pays per day ($100, $150, $200, $300/day, etc.)
- Insurance pays LOWER of: actual cost OR daily benefit amount
- Should match local LTC costs

**1.2 Benefit Period**
- How long benefits last (2 years, 3 years, 5 years, lifetime)
- Total maximum benefit = Daily benefit × 365 × Years
- Most people choose 3-5 years

**1.3 Elimination Period**
- Waiting period before insurance starts paying (30, 60, 90, 180 days)
- Like deductible but measured in time, not money
- During elimination period, insured pays out of pocket
- Longer elimination period = Lower premium

**1.4 Inflation Protection**
- Options: No inflation, simple inflation, compound inflation (3% or 5%)
- Critical feature: Without inflation, $200/day benefit in 30 years still only $200/day
- 3% compound inflation protects purchasing power

### 2. When to Buy LTC Insurance

**Key Concept: The "Goldilocks Zone" (Ages 50-65)**

**Too Young (<50):**
- Pay premiums for 30-40 years
- Total premiums might exceed benefits
- Low risk of needing LTC

**Optimal (50-65, especially 55-60):** ✓
- Premiums affordable ($2K-$3K/year at 55, $4K-$5K at 65)
- Most people healthy enough to qualify
- Best cost-benefit balance

**Too Late (>70):**
- Premiums very expensive ($6K-$8K+/year)
- Many have health issues → can't qualify or rated up
- Better to self-fund at this point

**Health Requirements:**
- Requires medical underwriting
- Can't qualify if: diabetes complications, heart disease, Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, stroke history, mobility issues

### 3. Self-Funding vs LTC Insurance Decision Framework

**Good Candidates for Self-Funding:**
1. Very wealthy (>$3-5M assets) - can easily afford $100K-$150K/year
2. Very low assets (<$100K-$200K) - can't afford premiums, will qualify for Medicaid quickly
3. Health issues - can't qualify for insurance

**Good Candidates for LTC Insurance:**
- Middle-class clients ($300K-$3M assets)
- "Sweet spot": $500K-$1.5M retirement assets
- Have enough to lose, not enough to self-fund comfortably
- Can afford $2K-$5K/year premiums
- Want to protect assets for spouse/heirs

### 4. Medicaid Planning

#### 4.1 Medicaid Eligibility

**Asset Limits (2025-2026):**
- Single: <$2,000-$2,500 in countable assets
- Married: <$3,000-$4,000 combined (varies by state)

**Exempt Assets (Don't count):**
- ✓ Primary home (up to ~$1M equity)
- ✓ One vehicle
- ✓ Personal belongings
- ✓ Prepaid burial/funeral
- ✓ Small life insurance (<$1,500 face value)

**Countable Assets:**
- Savings, investments, vacation homes, rental properties, etc.

#### 4.2 Spend-Down Strategy

**Legal spend-down:**
- Pay off debts
- Home improvements (primary residence is exempt!)
- Buy exempt assets (car, prepaid funeral)
- Pay for LTC out of pocket until below asset limit

**Illegal (triggers penalties):**
- Give money to children to "hide" assets
- Transfer assets for less than fair market value
- Triggers 5-year lookback period

#### 4.3 5-Year Lookback Period (CRITICAL)

**How it works:**
- Medicaid looks back 5 years before application
- If transferred assets within 5 years → penalty period
- Penalty = Amount transferred ÷ Average monthly LTC cost in state
- During penalty period: INELIGIBLE for Medicaid, must pay out of pocket

### 5. Special Needs Trusts (SNT)

**The Problem:**
- Disabled person receives government benefits (SSI, Medicaid)
- Benefits have strict asset limits (~$2,000)
- Direct inheritance → loses benefits

**The Solution: Special Needs Trust**
- Trust holds money for disabled beneficiary
- Managed by trustee (family, professional, bank)
- Pays for SUPPLEMENTAL needs (not covered by government benefits)
- Money in trust doesn't count toward asset limit
- Beneficiary keeps SSI and Medicaid

**What Trust CAN Pay For (Supplemental):**
- ✓ Education, tutoring
- ✓ Recreation, vacations, entertainment
- ✓ Technology, specialized equipment
- ✓ Transportation (car, wheelchair van)
- ✓ Home furnishings beyond basic
- ✓ Therapy not covered by Medicaid

**What Trust CANNOT Pay For (Reduces Benefits):**
- ✗ Food (directly)
- ✗ Shelter/housing (directly)
- ✗ Basic medical care covered by Medicaid

**Mastery Level:** **Medium Confidence** - Concept introduced, not independently tested yet

### 6. ABLE Accounts

**ABLE = Achieving a Better Life Experience Act**

**What It Is:**
- Special savings account for people with disabilities
- Like 529 education account, but for disability expenses
- Tax-advantaged: grows tax-free, withdrawals tax-free for qualified expenses

**Eligibility:**
- Disability onset before age 26
- Meets SSA definition of disability

**Key Features:**
- Annual contribution limit: $18,000 (2024, indexed)
- First $100,000 doesn't count toward SSI asset limit ($2,000)
- Over $100,000: SSI suspended (Medicaid continues)
- One ABLE account per person

**Qualified Disability Expenses (QDE):**
- ✓ Education
- ✓ Housing
- ✓ Transportation
- ✓ Employment training
- ✓ Assistive technology
- ✓ Health care
- ✓ Financial management
- Basically: maintaining health, independence, quality of life

**Advantages over SNT:**
- Simpler - individual/family can manage (no trustee)
- More flexible - broader qualified expenses
- Disabled person has control

**Limitation:**
- Only $18K/year contribution limit - can't protect large inheritance
- For larger amounts, need SNT

**CFP Strategy: Use BOTH**
- ABLE Account for annual gifting and flexibility
- Special Needs Trust for large amounts (life insurance, estate inheritance)

**Mastery Level:** **Medium Confidence** - Concept introduced, not independently tested yet
