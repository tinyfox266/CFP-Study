2. **Bonds (债券)**
   - Definition: Lending money (IOU from company/government)

   - More tax-efficient
   - Trade throughout the day

5. **Diversification (分散投资)**
   - "Don't put all eggs in one basket"
   - Reduces unsystematic risk
   - Example: 100 companies vs 1 company

### D.28 - Types of Investment Risk

**Student's Initial Understanding:**
- Basic intuition about risk = probability of loss and expected returns
- No knowledge of specific risk type categories

**Concepts Explained:**

1. **Market Risk (Systematic Risk/系统性风险)**
   - Affects entire market (e.g., 2008 financial crisis)
   - CANNOT be diversified away
   - Example: Fed raises rates, whole market drops

2. **Business Risk (Unsystematic Risk/非系统性风险)**
   - Company-specific risk
   - CAN be diversified away
   - Example: Tesla CEO controversy affects only Tesla

3. **Interest Rate Risk (利率风险)**
   - Inverse relationship: Rates ↑ → Bond Prices ↓
   - Longer maturity bonds = higher interest rate risk
   - Example: 30-year bond drops more than 2-year bond when rates rise

4. **Inflation Risk (通货膨胀风险)**
   - Returns don't keep pace with inflation
   - Purchasing power declines
   - Example: 2% return with 4% inflation = -2% real return

5. **Credit Risk (Default Risk/信用风险)**
   - Borrower may not repay
   - Mainly affects bonds
   - Example: Corporate bond issuer goes bankrupt

6. **Liquidity Risk (流动性风险)**
   - Cannot sell investment quickly without loss
   - Example: Rare collectibles hard to sell fast

### D.29 - Market Cycles

**Student's Initial Understanding:**
- Familiar with basic concepts of bull market (牛市) and bear market (熊市)
- Understood bull = rising, bear = falling

**Concepts Explained:**

1. **Bull vs Bear Markets**
   - Bull Market: 20%+ rise from lows, avg 4-5 years, strong economy
   - Bear Market: 20%+ fall from highs, avg 9-18 months, recession/slowdown

2. **Four Economic Cycle Phases**
   - EXPANSION: GDP rising, unemployment falling, stocks rising
   - PEAK: Maximum output, full employment, inflation rising
   - CONTRACTION: GDP falling, unemployment rising, stocks falling (recession if 6+ months)
   - TROUGH: Lowest point, high unemployment, recovery begins

3. **Defensive vs Cyclical Stocks**
   - Defensive (防御性股票): Necessities - utilities, food, healthcare
     - Stable during recessions, slower growth in expansions
     - Best during contraction/recession
   - Cyclical (周期性股票): Discretionary/luxury - travel, restaurants, luxury goods
     - Rise fast in expansions, fall hard in recessions
     - Best during expansion

4. **Economic Indicators**
   - Leading Indicators (领先指标): Change BEFORE economy - stock market, building permits, consumer confidence
   - Coincident Indicators (同步指标): Change WITH economy - GDP, employment, personal income
   - Lagging Indicators (滞后指标): Change AFTER economy - unemployment rate, corporate profits

**Student's Comprehension Checks:**

### D.30 - Quantitative Investment Concepts and Measures of Investment Returns

**Student's Initial Understanding:**
- Strong intuition that risk and return should be proportional
- Understood need to compare investments on risk-adjusted basis
- No knowledge of specific quantitative measures

**Concepts Explained:**

1. **Standard Deviation (标准差)**
   - Measures total volatility/risk
   - How much returns "bounce around"
   - Lower = more stable/predictable
   - Higher = wild swings
   - 68-95-99.7 Rule for normal distribution

2. **Beta (β贝塔系数)**
   - Measures systematic/market risk
   - Beta = 1.0: Moves exactly with market
   - Beta > 1.0: More volatile (aggressive)
   - Beta < 1.0: Less volatile (defensive)
   - Example: Market drops 20%, Beta 0.8 stock drops 16%, Beta 1.5 stock drops 30%

3. **Sharpe Ratio (夏普比率)**
   - Measures risk-adjusted return
   - Formula: (Return - Risk-free Rate) / Standard Deviation
   - HIGHER = BETTER (more return per unit of risk)
   - Key insight: Higher return doesn't always mean better investment

4. **Alpha (α阿尔法)**
   - Measures excess return beyond market expectations
   - Positive alpha = manager adding value (beating market)
   - Negative alpha = manager destroying value (underperforming)
   - Zero alpha = matching market (index fund)

5. **R-squared (R² 决定系数)**
   - Measures correlation to benchmark (0-100 or 0-1.0)
   - High R² (85+) = portfolio tracks benchmark closely → Beta is reliable
   - Low R² (<70) = portfolio doesn't track benchmark → Beta is unreliable

**Student's Comprehension Checks:**

### D.32 - Bond and Stock Valuation Concepts

**Student's Initial Knowledge:**
- No prior knowledge of bond or stock valuation metrics
- Understood basic concept that same payment at lower price = better value

**Concepts Explained:**

**BOND VALUATION:**

1. **Coupon Rate vs Current Yield vs Yield to Maturity (YTM)**
   - Coupon Rate: Fixed rate printed on bond, never changes
   - Current Yield: Annual interest / Market price (changes as price changes)
   - YTM: Total return if held to maturity (includes interest + capital gain/loss)

2. **Premium, Discount, and Par Bonds**
   - Par: Price = Face value ($1,000), Current Yield = Coupon Rate
   - Discount: Price < Face value, Current Yield > Coupon Rate, YTM > Current Yield
   - Premium: Price > Face value, Coupon Rate > Current Yield > YTM
   - Key insight: For discount bonds, you profit at maturity; for premium bonds, you lose

3. **Yield Hierarchy**
   - Discount bond: YTM > Current Yield > Coupon Rate (capital gain boosts YTM)
   - Premium bond: Coupon Rate > Current Yield > YTM (capital loss reduces YTM)
   - Par bond: All three equal

**STOCK VALUATION:**

1. **P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)**
   - Formula: P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)
   - Interpretation: How many dollars you pay for every $1 of earnings
   - Low P/E (5-15): Value stocks, potentially undervalued or slow growth
   - Medium P/E (15-25): Market average, fairly valued
   - High P/E (25+): Growth stocks, expensive but high growth expected
   - Must compare within same industry

2. **PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)**
   - Formula: PEG = P/E Ratio / Expected Growth Rate (%)
   - Adjusts P/E for growth expectations
   - PEG < 1.0: Potentially undervalued (getting growth at a discount)
   - PEG = 1.0: Fairly valued (paying appropriate price for growth)
   - PEG > 1.0: Potentially overvalued (overpaying for growth)
   - Logic: If company grows at 20%, P/E of 20 is "fair" (PEG = 1.0)
   - Higher growth justifies higher P/E

3. **Dividend Yield**
   - Formula: Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend / Stock Price
   - Measures cash income percentage from stock
   - High yield (4-7%): Income stocks (utilities, REITs, mature companies)
   - Low/No yield (0-2%): Growth stocks (reinvest profits for growth)
   - Warning: Very high yield (8%+) might signal stock price crash or trouble
   - Different investors prefer different yields (retirees want high, young investors prefer growth)
