#### Key Concepts Explained

**1. The CFP Board's 7-Step Financial Planning Process**

**Step 1: Understanding the Client's Personal and Financial Circumstances**
- Gather quantitative data (income, assets, debts, etc.)
- Gather qualitative data (goals, values, risk tolerance, family situation)

**Step 2: Identifying and Selecting Goals**
- Help client clarify and prioritize goals
- Make goals realistic and specific (e.g., "Retire at 65 with $80K/year" not "retire comfortably")

**Step 3: Analyzing Current Course + Potential Alternatives**
- Evaluate current position vs desired position (gap analysis)
- Identify what happens if client changes nothing
- Research alternative strategies

**Step 4: Developing the Recommendation(s)**
- Create specific recommendations based on analysis
- Consider client's values, risk tolerance, circumstances

**Step 5: Presenting the Recommendation(s)**
- Communicate clearly to client
- Explain rationale, pros/cons, risks
- Get client agreement

**Step 6: Implementing the Recommendation(s)**
- Put plan into action
- May involve other professionals (attorneys, CPAs, insurance agents)

**Step 7: Monitoring Progress and Updating**
- Review plan regularly (annually or when life changes)
- Update recommendations as needed
- **Cycles back to Step 1 when major changes occur**

**2. Key Principle: Continuous Cycle**
- Financial planning is ongoing, not one-time
- Life changes trigger plan updates (job loss, marriage, kids, etc.)

**3. Professional Standards**
- Must complete Steps 1-3 (gather data and analyze) BEFORE Step 4 (recommend)
- Never recommend without proper analysis
- Monitoring (Step 7) is a professional duty, not optional

#### Key Concepts Explained

**1. Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position)**

**The Equation:** Assets - Liabilities = Net Worth

**Assets** (What you OWN) - organized by liquidity:
1. Cash & Cash Equivalents (checking, savings, money market)
2. Invested Assets (stocks, bonds, mutual funds, 401k, IRA)
3. Use Assets (home, car, furniture, jewelry)
4. Other Assets (business ownership, real estate investments, collectibles)

**Liabilities** (What you OWE):
1. Short-term liabilities (credit cards, bills, taxes due within 1 year)
2. Long-term liabilities (mortgage, student loans, car loans)

**Net Worth** = Total wealth/financial position

**2. Income Statement (Cash Flow Statement)**

**The Equation:** Income - Expenses = Net Cash Flow

**Income** (Money IN):
- Salary, business income, investment dividends, rental income

**Expenses** (Money OUT):
- Housing, food, transportation, utilities, entertainment, debt payments

**Net Cash Flow** = Amount left over to save or invest (or deficit if negative)

**3. How Statements Work Together**
- Balance Sheet = Snapshot at one point in time ðŸ“¸
- Income Statement = Flow over time period ðŸŽ¥
- Positive cash flow â†’ Net worth grows over time
- Negative cash flow â†’ Net worth shrinks (going into debt)

**4. Key CFP Analysis Ratios**

**Liquidity Ratio** = Cash & Liquid Assets Ã· Monthly Expenses
- Target: 3-6 months (6-12 if single income/self-employed)
- Measures emergency fund adequacy

**Savings Ratio** = Monthly Savings Ã· Gross Monthly Income
- Target: At least 10-20%
- Measures wealth-building rate

**Debt-to-Asset Ratio** = Total Liabilities Ã· Total Assets
- Target: Below 50%
- Measures leverage level

**Housing Ratio** = Monthly Housing Costs Ã· Gross Monthly Income
- Target: Below 28%
- Measures housing affordability

#### Key Concepts Explained

**1. The 50/30/20 Rule**
- 50% â†’ Needs (housing, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, minimum debt payments)
- 30% â†’ Wants (dining out, entertainment, shopping, hobbies, travel)
- 20% â†’ Savings & Debt Payoff (emergency fund, retirement, extra debt payments, investments)
- Most popular and easiest framework for clients

**2. Pay Yourself First**
- Save BEFORE spending, not after
- Correct: Income â†’ Savings First â†’ Expenses
- Wrong: Income â†’ Expenses â†’ Whatever's left â†’ Savings
- Automate transfers to guarantee savings

**3. Zero-Based Budgeting**
- Principle: Every dollar has a job
- Income - All Assigned Expenses = $0
- Intentional spending, no "leaks"
- Good for detail-oriented clients

**4. Envelope System**
- Cash-based budgeting for variable expenses
- Put cash in envelopes for each category
- When envelope empty, stop spending
- Psychological advantage: Cash feels more "real" than credit cards

**5. Fixed vs Variable Expenses**
- Fixed: Same amount monthly (rent, car payment, insurance)
- Variable: Changes monthly (food, utilities, entertainment)
- Strategy: Use 3-6 month average for variable expense budgeting

**6. Credit Card Management**
- Credit cards are TOOLS, not extra money
- Pay full balance monthly to avoid 18-25% interest
- Use for rewards/tracking if disciplined
- If can't pay full balance â†’ stop using, switch to debit/cash

**7. Client-Specific Strategies**
- "Don't know where money goes" â†’ Track for 30 days, identify leaks
- "Can't save money" â†’ Pay Yourself First, automate savings
- "Irregular income" â†’ Budget on lowest month, build 6-12 month emergency fund

**8. Financial Planning Hierarchy of Priorities**
- Tier 1 (SURVIVAL): Emergency fund, pay off high-interest debt, adequate insurance
- Tier 2 (BUILDING WEALTH): Employer 401k match, pay off moderate debt, retirement savings
- Tier 3 (OPTIMIZATION): Tax optimization, college savings, advanced strategies

**9. The Balanced Approach to Debt vs Savings**
- NEVER choose one over the other
- Always do BOTH simultaneously
- Split extra money between debt payoff AND emergency fund building
- Once emergency fund adequate (3 months), can be more aggressive on debt
- Once debt paid off, accelerate emergency fund to 6 months
